Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Common Application?

The Common Application is an undergraduate college admission application that applicants may use to apply to any of more than 800 member colleges and universities in 49 states and the District of Columbia, as well as in Canada, China, and many European countries,

Basically, it'll be your one-stop-shop for submitting most of your applications, essays, and reccomendations.

What is the Coalition Application?

The Coalition Application and myCoalition are free, online college planning tools that helps students learn about, prepare for, and apply to college.
It is also a website that allows for submission of applications, essays, and recommendations.

Most schools will be available on both the Coalition Application and the Common Application but be sure to research that information before you begin applying.

What are SAT Subject Tests?

Subject tests are 60-minute tests administered by the College Board at your testing center. They are on one topic and a great way to demonstrate proficiency in one subject. Some schools require subject tests in your program of interest. Research your college to find out those requirements.

For information about fee waivers for SAT Subject Tests, speak to your guidance counselor or visit this site.

How do I know if I can afford a college?

Every college and university will have something called the Net Price Calculator on their website. Before you apply to a school, go on to their website and find this calculator. You will be prompted to enter basic tax and income information and it will output your expected family contribution, or EFC.
The FAFSA or Free Application for Federal Student Aid will take this same information and alert you to what assistance is available to you and your family.

An example of a Net Price calculator can be found here and the FASFA website is here.

Will AP or IB look better on my applications?

It does not matter. Colleges would like to see that you took the most challenging courses for you. If those are IB courses, that's awesome. If those are AP courses, dope!
You are judged against people from your school in most cases, not people in different districts and with different opportunities.

What do early action and early decision mean?

There are three different types of application cycles: regular decision, rolling, and early programs.

With rolling decisions, you will find out the decision made on your application within a few weeks of submitting your application. The benefit of this is that you will have the decision on your application sooner rather than later. However, this also means that the later you submit an application, the chances of acceptance are lower.

With early programs, there are two main categories. Early action is nonbinding - meaning you are not obligated to attend if you are accepted - and you will recieve your decision in around January or December. Early action is generally binding - meaning you will be required to go. However, for both of these, the application deadline is around Early November.

Regular decision means you will likely not turn in your application until January 1st. You will recieve a decision sometime in the Spring but you are not obligated to attend the university.

I have no idea what to write my college essay on. What should I do?

Anything. Write about anything that matters to you. Are you really passionate about sketch comedy? Write about it. Do you enjoy birdwatching? Write about it. The goal is that anyone who knows you can read your essay without your name on it and know who wrote it.

Just avoid writing an essay about something because you think "[X] school will love this essay" or "this essay makes me sound like real student at [X]." Write about yourself because as much as this process is you applying for a school, the schools are looking for people who would be a good fit with the student body.
